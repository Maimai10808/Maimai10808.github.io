Definition:

An escaping closure is a closure that is passed into a function as a parameter, but may be called after the function has already returned.

When declaring a function that takes a closure parameter, you use the @escaping keyword to indicate that the closure is allowed to escape the function body.

This means the closure ‚Äúescapes‚Äù the function‚Äôs body.

‚Ö¢. What Does @escaping Do?

By default, Swift closures passed as parameters are non-escaping, meaning:

But for asynchronous operations, like network requests:

The network request is sent immediately, but the closure that handles the response is executed at a later time ‚Äî that‚Äôs why it must be marked as an escaping closure (@escaping).

‚Ö¶.Limitations of Escaping Closures

While escaping closures are powerful and necessary for handling asynchronous operations, they come with certain risks ‚Äî particularly around memory management.

Risk of Retain Cycles

One of the most important caveats when using @escaping closures is the potential for retain cycles. Since escaping closures are stored and executed later, they can strongly capture self, preventing it from being deallocated.

Best Practice:

Be careful: Escaping closures can capture self strongly, which may lead to retain cycles.
To avoid memory leaks, use [weak self] or [unowned self] when referencing self inside escaping closures.

‚Ö®.Summary

We can think of a running program as a car cruising on a highway.

When everything is synchronous‚Äîno network requests, no delayed tasks‚Äîit‚Äôs like driving straight down a clear, straight road. You don‚Äôt have to steer much or worry about detours; everything proceeds predictably and smoothly.

However, the moment you introduce asynchronous operations‚Äîlike network calls, timers, or background processing‚Äîthings change. It‚Äôs as if the road starts to curve. Now, you have to turn the wheel, adjust your path, and sometimes even wait at intersections for responses before moving forward.

In this scenario, the closures you pass into functions can no longer be executed immediately. They ‚Äúescape‚Äù the straight road of the function and are triggered later, when the response arrives. These closures must be marked with @escaping to let the Swift compiler know:

‚ÄúThis closure won‚Äôt finish instantly‚Äîit will leave the function and come back later. Keep it safe until then.‚Äù

So at its core, the purpose of @escaping is:

To allow delayed tasks the space and permission to live beyond the function‚Äôs lifetime, so they can be safely executed when the time is right.
